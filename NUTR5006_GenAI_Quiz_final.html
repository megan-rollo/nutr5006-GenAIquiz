Responsible Use of Generative AI in Research

This self-assessment quiz is designed to help you check your understanding of the responsible and ethical use of Generative Artificial Intelligence (GenAI) tools in research before you begin your NUTR5006 project.

The quiz covers the key principles set out in the NUTR5006 Guidelines for the Appropriate Use of Generative Artificial Intelligence (2026), including when and how GenAI tools may be used across the research process, what is never appropriate, and your obligations around disclosure, record-keeping, privacy, and copyright. It also draws on the Curtin University HDR GenAI Guidelines and the NHMRC Guide for Assessing Research Involving AI. These documents and other additional resources are available on Blackboard.

There are 16 questions covering five areas: literature discovery and synthesis, data analysis and interpretation, manuscript writing, responsible GenAI use, and supervisor approval and record-keeping.

If you select an incorrect answer, you will see the feedback and have one opportunity to try again. The correct answer will be revealed if the second attempt is also incorrect.

You need to answer all 16 questions correctly to complete the quiz — if you do not achieve a perfect score, you will be asked to retake it.

As a refresher before you start the quiz, a brief summary of the key sections of the Guidelines is provided below. Expand each section by clicking on it.

GenAI tools are artificial intelligence systems that generate new content, such as text, images, code and summaries, in response to user prompts. Many use Large Language Models (LLMs), which generate responses by identifying patterns in large collections of text rather than by understanding or verifying information in the way a human researcher would.

Not all AI tools are the same. Generic tools such as ChatGPT, Microsoft Copilot, Gemini and Claude are designed for general use and can assist with writing, summarising, and communication tasks. Specialist research tools such as Semantic Scholar and ResearchRabbit are designed specifically for literature discovery and citation mapping, and are generally more reliable for those tasks than generic tools. Regardless of the tool used, always verify AI-generated content against original sources before use.

LLMs generate plausible-sounding output but cannot verify whether information is accurate, current, or correctly attributed. Always verify all AI-generated content against original sources.

GenAI tools may be used in this unit in accordance with Curtin University's Academic and Research Integrity principles. If you wish to use GenAI tools, you must first discuss their proposed use with your supervisor(s) or unit coordinator and obtain approval before proceeding.

If using GenAI tools in the research process, remember:

  • GenAI can support learning, writing, and communication, but must not replace your own analysis and interpretation, judgement, or decision-making.
  • You remain responsible for the accuracy, originality, and authenticity of all submitted work.
  • GenAI-generated outputs must be reviewed, edited, and checked by you before use.
  • GenAI tools cannot be listed as authors of academic work.
  • Any use of GenAI must be transparently disclosed, including the tool used, how it was used, and the prompts provided.
  • You must keep a log of your GenAI use to support transparency in research and assessment reporting.

You must never upload the following to commercial GenAI tools:

  • Any data relating to research participants, including individuals, organisations, and services, even if the data has been deidentified. Deidentification does not eliminate re-identification risk.
  • Copyrighted materials, including copies or files of journal articles, guidelines, and reports. Downloading a PDF for personal research is permitted; uploading it to a third-party cloud GenAI service is a separate act not covered by most publisher licences.
  • Confidential, sensitive, private, or personal information, including your own.
These prohibitions apply at all stages of the research process and without exception. Always check publisher licence agreements before uploading any document to a GenAI platform.

Literature discovery
Use tools developed specifically for literature discovery, such as Semantic Scholar and ResearchRabbit, rather than generic tools like ChatGPT. Run parallel Boolean searches in databases such as PubMed, PsycINFO, CINAHL, and Web of Science, as AI tools may not fully index these sources.

Understanding and synthesising the literature
The most effective approach for establishing your understanding of a new area is to take your own notes using an evidence matrix.

The evidence matrix combines the functions of a traditional evidence table and a thematic matrix into a single tool. It organises individual studies in rows with standardised columns for study characteristics and findings, and extends this with columns for comparing similarities and differences across sources and a notes column for emerging observations. This built-in comparative layer supports synthesis without requiring a separate tool, making it useful for both formal reviews and for developing your understanding of a new area. GenAI can help you design the column structure, but all cells must be populated from your own reading. The analytical work of identifying similarities and differences across sources must be your own.

GenAI-generated summaries are not a substitute for reading. Relying on a tool's summary without reading the original paper is an academic integrity issue — you cannot vouch for accuracy you have not verified yourself.

Data analysis and interpretation
Uploading research data, including deidentified data, is not permitted under any circumstances. You may use GenAI to help think through how to process your data without sharing actual data. For quantitative work, share structure and context about your statistical approach only. For qualitative work, describe your analysis approach or emerging themes in your own words. Discuss any interactions with GenAI tools with your supervisor(s) before proceeding.

Manuscript writing
GenAI may support your written expression but must not replace your own analysis, judgement, or decision-making. Intellectual ownership, verification, and transparent disclosure apply in full. Any use must be clearly acknowledged as set out in the assessment instructions.

All use of GenAI tools must be transparently disclosed in your assessments and manuscript. Your disclosure should include:

  • The name of the tool used
  • How it was used
  • The prompts provided (where relevant)

Journals require acknowledgement of any GenAI use. It is therefore essential that you document all GenAI interactions throughout the research process by keeping a log. This log supports transparency in assessment reporting and will be required if your work is submitted for publication.

Check the instructions for each assessment for specific guidance on how to acknowledge GenAI use in that submission.
